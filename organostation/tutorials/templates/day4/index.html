Sample preparation

This guide is the most important part of the tutorials, and it will guide you through the preparation of the organoid and the recording procedure. Neural tissue has special properties that make it very sensitive to changes in mechano-chemical conditions. Thus, special care is required when handling the preparation.

1. Tissue immobilization

One of the most important elements in an electrical recording is mechanical stability. We will use 4% (wt/vol) low-melt agarose (Biozym, Cat. No. 850080) in phosphate-buffered solution (PBS) to immobilize the organoid within the recording chamber. Also, artificial cerebrospinal fluid (ACSF) will be delivered at a rate of 2-3 mL/min while keeping both solution and chamber near physiological temperature (30-35 °C ). When keeping mechanical stability, the composition of the fluid, and the temperature of the tissue, we can guarantee recordings under neurophysiological conditions. 

1.1 Sample preparation

We recommend using the recording station close to the culture room to avoid mechanical stress on the organoids. Before starting the procedure, have a bottle (~, e.g., 500 mL) of ACSF gassed with carbogen for at least 20 minutes to equilibrate the pH solution (~ 7.25-7.35) at room temperature.

Once taken from the incubator, cerebral organoids are to keep at near-physiological temperature (30-35 °C ) in ACSF for ten minutes (e.g., in a 35-mm petri dish). While keeping the organoids in ACSF, it is recommended to melt the 4% (wt/vol) low-melt agarose (Biozym, Cat. No. 850080) in phosphate-buffered solution (PBS) in a thermal block (or in thermal cycler). At the same time, we can fill a 10 x 10 mL plastic container with ice, and cover it with a paper blanket. We will operate the recording chamber on top of the blanket. In this way, the chamber is very cold, but not frozen. At both the left and right sides of the chamber, we will have preparation tools (like pipettes of different sizes, filter paper, etc. see below).

The preparation must be visible under a bench-top magnifier or low-power microscope, as depicted below.

 

Here is a summary of the tools to have before the sample preparation:

  • ACSF bubbled in Carbogen for 20 minutes at room temperature.
  • Organoids in ACSF at near physiological temperature (30-35 °C ) in a 35 mm petri dish

1.2 Embedding the organoid

Now that we have the organoid on the dish, we will use the plastic Pasteur pipette (or an Eppendorf pipette tip) and cut it to have a ~ 5 mm diameter or larger. It allows us to get the organoid with some ACSF without touching the tissue. We use it to move it to the recording chamber on the preparation table. Note that the organoid will contain a few microliters (maybe up to 1 mL) of ACSF in the recording chamber. We will eliminate those with a 1 mL Eppendorf pipette. After that, use the filter paper (or Kim-wipe / absorbent swab) carefully, to remove the excess liquid around the border of the organoid. It is necessary that the border is almost dry to allow the agar to surround the tissue and fix it together with the chamber. After it, we will add 4% melted agarose in the surroundings of the organoid, that in contact with the frozen chamber, will solidify immediately and fix the organoid to the chamber. This is when we add ~ 2 mL of ACSF at room temperature, to finally move the chamber to the organostation, and start the perfusion of warm (~ 35 °C) ACSF with the peristaltic pump.

Here is the whole process summarized.

  1. Move the organoid for the 35 mm petri dish to the recording chamber.
  2. Remove the excess ACSF with an Eppendorf pipette, and dry out the borders of the organoid with filter paper.
  3. Add immediately 194 microliters of melted 4% agar around the borders of the organoid.
  4. Add ACSF to cover the whole volume of the organoid (about 2 mL).
  5. Move the chamber carefully to the organostation and start the perfusion of warm (~ 35 °C) ACSF

The most critical step is drying out the border of the organoids. Neural tissue will immediately die if not in the presence of ACSF. Therefore, the organoid must be kept in ACSF as long as possible.

2. Electrode penetration

Once the chamber is in the organostation, look carefully if the organoid remains embedded in agar. If yes, it is time to take the probes and insert them into the adaptor for the micromanipulators. For that, you need to move the manipulators at a fast speed outside of the recording chamber. Once there, inset the probes on the manipulators, and do not forget to allocate the reference electrode inside the bath (see figure). 

After setting the grounding electrode, move the manipulators fast until you see it under the microscope. At the same time, monitor the noise of the recordings in your computer. Noise is very large if the electrodes are outside ACSF. Once the electrodes are on top of the organoid, select a slower speed and move downwards. At the moment you touch the surface of the organoid, you will see that the noise of the electrodes increases slightly. This is the moment when you set the counter to zero in the manipulator pad, and move the probe using the wheel. Insert now the probes with the electrodes around 200- 300 micrometers following the display in the manipulator pad.

3. Cleaning protocol

Ideally, you would like to start and end cleaning the probe by immersing it in a 1% (w/v) tergazyme in dH2O solution at ~50 degrees for 20 minutes. After that, you can remove any debris with a small brush under visual inspection (e.g., bench-top magnifier). 

  1. Immersion in 1% (w/v) tergazyme at ~ 50 °C for approximately 20 minutes.
  2. Rinse abundantly with bi-distilled water at ~ 50 °C  
  3. Rinse with isopropanol at room temperature 

3.1 Probe cleaning

Once the acquisition is made, and the binary file is stored, you must operate carefully to remove the probes from the manipulator. During this time, the tissue can remain at physiological temperature and with the proper recirculation in the chamber. It helps if you require to perform a new recording on a different day and recover after the brief immersion of the probes. To remove the probes, move the manipulator to the furthest possible position, and once there, far away from the tissue, remove the PCB that contains the probe.

Always move the probe upwards, avoiding any contact. Securely mount your probe into a moveable stand to soak the probe shank(s) in a heated magnetic stirrer bath (cleaning station, see figure on the left). Insert the probe in 1% (w/v) tergazyme in dH2O solution at ~50 degrees for approximately 20 minutes. Alternatively, you can use Trypsin 10X (Cat. No, 59427C from Sigma) diluted 1:10 in distilled water and soak the probe shank for a few hours at 35 degrees Celsius.

Finally, you can store the probe within a Falcon Tube containing 30 mL of dH2O.
