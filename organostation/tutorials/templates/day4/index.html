Sample preparation

This guide is the most important part of the tutorials, and it will guide you through the preparation of the organoid for the recording procedure. Neural tissue has special properties that make it sensitive to changes in mechano-chemical conditions. Thus, special care is required when handling the preparation.

1. Tissue immobilization

One of the most important elements in an electrical recording is mechanical stability. We will use 4% (wt/vol) low-melt agarose (Biozym, Cat. No. 850080) in phosphate-buffered solution (PBS) to immobilize the organoid within the recording chamber. Also, artificial cerebrospinal fluid (ACSF) will be delivered at a rate of 2-3 mL/min while keeping both solution and chamber near physiological temperature (30-35 °C ). When keeping mechanical stability, the composition of the fluid, and the temperature of the tissue, we can guarantee recordings are performed under neurophysiological conditions.

1.1 Preparation table

We recommend having the recording station close to the culture room to avoid mechanical stress on the organoids. Before starting the procedure, have a bottle (e.g., 500 mL) of ACSF gassed with carbogen for at least 20 minutes to equilibrate the pH solution (~ 7.25-7.35) at room temperature.

Once taken from the incubator, cerebral organoids are to keep at near-physiological temperature (30-35 °C ) in ACSF for ten minutes (e.g., in a 35-mm petri dish). While keeping the organoids in ACSF, it is recommended to melt the 4% low-melt agarose (using a thermal block, or in a thermal cycler). At the same time, we can fill a 10 x 10 mL plastic container with ice, and cover it with a paper blanket. We will allocate the recording chamber on top of the blanket. In this way, the chamber is very cold, but not frozen. On both sides of the chamber, we will have preparation tools (like pipettes of different sizes, filter paper, etc. see below).

The preparation table must be visible under a bench-top magnifier or low-power microscope, as depicted below.

Here is a summary of the tools to have on the preparation table:

  • ACSF bubbled in Carbogen for 20 minutes at room temperature.
  • Organoids in ACSF at near physiological temperature (30-35 °C ) in a 35 mm petri dish
  • 1.5 mL Eppendorf containing 4% agar melted in a thermal block at around 50 °C 
  • A plastic cube (~ 10 x 10 cm) filled with ice
  • Preparation tools:
    • 1) 1mL and 200 microL eppendorf pipettes
    • 2) 5 mL syringe
    • 3) Plastic Pasteur Pipette
    • 5) Dissecting needle
    • 4) Filter paper (e.g., Watmann) or Kim-wipe / absorbent swab

1.2 Embedding the organoid

Now that we have the organoid on the dish, we will use the plastic Pasteur pipette (or an Eppendorf pipette tip) and cut it to obtain a ~ 5 mm diameter tip or larger. It allows us to get the organoid with some ACSF without touching the tissue. We use it to move from the petri dish to the recording chamber on the preparation table. Note that the organoid will contain a few microliters (maybe up to 1 mL) of ACSF in the recording chamber. We will eliminate those with a 1 mL Eppendorf pipette. After that, use the filter paper (or Kim-wipe / absorbent swab) carefully, to remove the excess liquid around the border of the organoid. It is essential to dry the border to allow the agar to surround the tissue and fix it together with the chamber. After it, we will add ~200 microliters of 4% melted agarose in the surroundings of the organoid, that in contact with the frozen chamber, will solidify immediately. This is when we add ~ 2 mL of ACSF at room temperature with the plastic Pasteur pipette. Once immersed in ACSF, the organoid on the recording chamber must be transported to the organostation, and start the perfusion of warm (~ 35 °C) ACSF with the peristaltic pump.

Here is the whole process summarized.

  1. Move the organoid for the 35 mm petri dish to the recording chamber.
  2. Remove the excess ACSF with an Eppendorf pipette, and dry out the borders of the organoid with filter paper.
  3. Add immediately ~ 200 uL of melted 4% agar around the borders of the organoid.
  4. Cover the organoid with warm ACSF (about 2 mL).
  5. Move the chamber carefully to the organostation and start the perfusion of warm (~ 35 °C) ACSF

The most critical step is drying out the border of the organoids. Neural tissue will immediately die and suffer from anoxia if not in the presence of ACSF. Therefore, the organoid must be kept in ACSF as long as possible.

2. Electrode penetration

Once the chamber is in the organostation, look carefully if the organoid remains embedded in the agar block. If yes, it is time to take the probes and insert them into the adaptor for the micromanipulators. For that, you need to move the manipulators at a fast speed outside of the recording chamber. Once there, inset the probes on the manipulators, and do not forget to allocate the reference electrode inside the bath (see figure).

After setting the grounding electrode, move the manipulators fast until you see them under the microscope. At the same time, monitor the noise of the recordings on your computer. Noise is very large if the electrodes are outside ACSF. Once the electrodes are on top of the organoid, select a slower speed and move downwards. The moment you touch the surface of the organoid, you will see that the noise of the electrodes increases slightly. This is when you set the counter to zero in the manipulator pad, and move the probe using the wheel very slowly (~ 10 um per cycle). Monitor that you insert the probes  ~ 200 - 300 micrometers deep in the organoid, because that's where active neurons migrate during the development of organoids. Deeper insertions may contain stem cells and neural progenitors, or reach necrotic areas that do not have active electrical properties.

2.1 Documenting recordings

Once the desired location is reached, wait for ~10-25 minutes to let the tissue recover. You can monitor the oscilloscope in the presence of extracellular spikes appearing spontaneously in closed electrode locations. Note that small movements may activate close-by neurons, and electrical artifacts (e.g., switches, steppers, etc.) may contribute to electrical signals sampled by electrodes but are not from neurons.

Once you can reproduce the presence of extracellular spiking activity around a group of electrodes, you can start the recording and monitor the location - we recommend recording at least 10 minutes-. Once the acquisition is made, and the binary file is stored, you must operate carefully to remove the probes from the manipulator. During this time, the tissue can remain at physiological temperature and with the proper recirculation in the chamber. It helps if you require to perform a new recording on a different day and recover after the brief immersion of the probes. To remove the probes, move the manipulator to the furthest possible position, and once there, far away from the tissue, remove the PCB that contains the probe.

3. Cleaning protocol

Ideally, you would like to start and end cleaning the experiment with a cleaning procedure to eliminate contamination from your probes. You can clean the probes by immersing them in a 1% (w/v) tergazyme in dH2O solution at ~50 degrees for 20 minutes. After that, you can remove debris with a small brush under visual inspection (e.g., bench-top magnifier) that contains abundant drops of bi-distilled water. 

  1. Immersion in 1% (w/v) tergazyme at ~ 50 °C for approximately 20 minutes.
  2. Rinse abundantly with bi-distilled water at ~ 50 °C  (brush if necessary)

3.1 Probe cleaning and storage

To clean the probe before storage, you will need a cleaning station (see the image below) that contains a moveable stand to soak the probe shank(s) in a heated magnetic stirrer bath. Insert the probes first in 1% tergazyme at ~50 degrees for approximately 20 minutes (*), and clean them as described in the cleaning protocol. This time, we add a last step that includes treatment in iso-propanol before storage in a Falcon Tube containing 30 mL of distilled H2O.

  1. Immersion in 1% (w/v) tergazyme at ~ 50 °C for approximately 20 minutes.
  2. Rinse abundantly with bi-distilled water at ~ 50 °C  (brush if necessary).
  3. Brush visually with iso-propanol at room temperature.
  4. Storage of probes in Falcon Tube in distilled water. 

Alternatively,you can use Trypsin 10X (Cat. No, 59427C from Sigma) diluted 1:10 in distilled water to soak the probe shanks for a few hours at 35 degrees Celsius.
